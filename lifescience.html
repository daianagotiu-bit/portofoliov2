Life Science & Healthcare 12 months Web Application

Product Vision & Business Context

The Market Problem: Life sciences researchers struggled with cloud infrastructure complexity. Running computational analysis required deep technical knowledge of AWS, Docker, and environment configuration, creating a barrier between scientists and the tools they needed.

Product Vision: Democratize cloud computing for scientists. Make launching complex computational environments as simple as ordering a coffee, technical complexity hidden behind intuitive interfaces.

Business Goals: Reduce time to first analysis from 3 days to 30 minutes. Increase cloud adoption among non-technical scientists by 60%. Enable self-service infrastructure that reduces support ticket volume by 40%.

Target Users: Bioinformaticians (primary), research scientists with basic computing skills (secondary), IT administrators needing oversight capabilities (tertiary). Ages 30 to 55, varying technical confidence levels.

Design Challenges & Solutions
Breaking down technical complexity

Challenge: Make complex cloud workflows accessible to non-technical users

Solution: Introduced guided carousel wizard for creating sessions, environments, and jobs. Visual step by step approach improved user comprehension.

Supporting power users

Challenge: Provide customization without overwhelming simple users

Solution: Advanced configuration panel with toggle between simple and advanced modes, hidden unless needed.

Visual identity differentiation

Challenge: Stand out from typical gray lab apps

Solution: Bold dark mode design reducing eye strain while creating modern, distinct visual tone.

Technology: Tailwind CSS

Outcome

Delivered a web app that both technical and non-technical users can easily navigate, streamlining complex laboratory tasks while enhancing user engagement with visually distinct dark mode. The carousel wizard approach improved workflow efficiency by breaking intricate setups into digestible steps. The bold dark mode shift not only stood out against conventional lab software but also reduced visual fatigue, resulting in increased adoption and improved user experience for power users managing large data sets.

What I Learned & Product Thinking Evolution
Progressive disclosure was the key

The insight: Early prototypes tried to show all options upfront, overwhelming users. I learned that guiding users through a journey (carousel wizard) felt less intimidating than a single complex form.

Product decision: Introduced "simple" vs "advanced" mode toggle. 80% of users never touched advanced settings, validating that most wanted quick setup, not customization.

Lesson: Product design isn't about showing everything, it's about revealing the right thing at the right time.

Dark mode, aesthetic choice that became business value

Initial reasoning: I pushed for dark mode because it looked modern and different from competitors' gray interfaces.

Unexpected win: User testing revealed scientists who stared at screens for 8+ hours loved it for reducing eye strain. What started as visual differentiation became a genuine usability advantage.

Learning: Sometimes design intuition hits on real user needs you didn't anticipate. Stay curious about why users respond positively.

Technical jargon was unavoidable (and that's okay)

Challenge: I initially tried to "simplify" all technical terms. Docker became "container", instances became "machines."

User pushback: Power users were confused. They knew the technical terms and wanted to see them. Oversimplification felt patronizing.

Solution: Used proper technical terms with tooltips explaining them. Let users grow into the platform rather than dumbing it down.

Product insight: Know your audience. Life sciences users are smart, they can learn technical concepts if you explain them well. Don't underestimate users.

Status indicators, the unsung hero

What worked better than expected: Simple color coded status dots (green=running, yellow=starting, red=error) on lab cards became the most used feature.

Why it mattered: Scientists didn't need to dig into details, they could scan the dashboard and instantly know "everything's fine" or "something needs attention."

Takeaway: Sometimes the smallest UI elements deliver the biggest value. At a glance information reduces cognitive load.

LabFlow

Healthcare web application for digitalization of laboratory experiments

Life Science & Healthcare 12 months Web Application

Product Vision & Business Context

The Problem: Laboratory experiments relied on manual data entry into paper notebooks and Excel sheets. This created data entry errors, made collaboration difficult, and prevented real-time analysis. Scientists spent 30% of their time on administrative tasks rather than actual research.

Product Opportunity: Transform the lab from analog to digital. Create a system where experiment data flows directly from instruments to a centralized platform, enabling real-time collaboration, automated analysis, and complete audit trails.

Business Impact Goals: Reduce data entry errors by 90%, decrease time to results by 40%, enable regulatory compliance with complete digital audit trails, and free up 20+ hours per week per scientist for actual research work.

User Personas: Lab technicians (daily data collectors), research scientists (data analyzers), and quality assurance teams (compliance auditors). Mix of tech-savvy younger staff and experienced scientists comfortable with traditional methods.

Design Challenges & Solutions
Understanding lab technology

Challenge: Grasping complex lab workflows and processes

Solution: Researched thoroughly and collaborated with lab technicians and scientists to map workflows and identify pain points.

Domain knowledge acquisition

Challenge: Learning specific lab procedures and chemical elements

Solution: Engaged in self-directed learning and consulted with experts to understand lab requirements.

Managing large data sets

Challenge: Designing user friendly interface for complex data

Solution: Applied information architecture principles for easy data access and usability.

Created comprehensive visual direction for lab digitalization platform

Designed high level navigation structure for complex workflows

Conducted testing sessions with real users and documented feedback

Outcome

The digitalization of laboratory experiments resulted in a streamlined process, reducing manual errors and improving data accessibility. Delivered a product that significantly improved scientists' daily tasks and contributed to lab operational success. Conducted usability testing sessions with real users, gathered feedback, and documented everything to ensure continuous improvement and alignment with user needs.

What I Learned & Honest Challenges
The learning curve was steep (for me)

Reality: I had zero knowledge of laboratory workflows, chemical elements, or scientific processes. I couldn't design for users whose world I didn't understand.

Solution: Shadowed lab technicians for full work days. Watched them conduct experiments, asked "dumb questions," and built mental models of their workflows.

Key learning: You can't design for users from behind a desk. Real observation beats assumptions every time. This project taught me the value of deep user immersion.

Change resistance was the real challenge

The unexpected barrier: Technical implementation was smooth. User adoption was hard. Senior scientists were attached to paper notebooks, they trusted physical records.

What didn't work: Emphasizing "efficiency" and "modern workflows." They didn't care about being modern.

What worked: Framing digital records as "more trustworthy" for regulatory audits and showing how it prevented data loss. Spoke to their values, not ours.

Product lesson: Feature benefits matter less than whether you're solving a problem users actually feel. Learn to speak their language.

Moderated usability testing changed everything

The turning point: Conducting moderated usability testing with real lab technicians revealed assumptions I'd made that were completely wrong.

Example: I assumed users would navigate chronologically through experiment steps. They actually jumped around constantly, checking previous steps and making adjustments.

Design change: Added a persistent navigation sidebar showing all experiment steps, with ability to jump to any step instantly. This became a core feature.

Learning: This was my first experience with formal usability testing, and it fundamentally changed how I approach design. Watch users struggle equals learn more than any stakeholder meeting.

Data visualization, initially overlooked

Early focus: I concentrated on data input, making it easy to record results.

User need: They actually cared more about data output, seeing trends, comparing experiments, and spotting anomalies.

Product pivot: Added visualization dashboards as a follow-up phase. This became more valuable than the input features.

Reflection: I learned to think about the complete user journey, not just the first interaction. What happens after users enter data matters just as much.
