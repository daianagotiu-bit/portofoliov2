Enterprise Workspace Platform

Revolutionizing internal collaboration tools

Internal Product 2024, Present Design Systems & AI

Product Vision & Strategic Context

The Business Challenge: An enterprise workspace platform serving thousands of internal colleagues across multiple product teams needed to evolve. Users struggled with fragmented search, unclear navigation, and lack of proactive intelligence. The platform worked... but it didn't delight or enable users to work smarter.

Product Vision: Transform the workspace from a passive tool into an intelligent assistant. Make every interaction feel effortless, where search anticipates needs, feeds surface relevant information automatically, and AI accelerates common workflows without feeling intrusive.

Strategic Goals: Reduce time to information by 50%, increase daily active usage by 30%, establish the platform as the primary entry point for work (not just another tool), and prove AI-enhanced workflows could reduce development cycles by 40%.

My Role: UX/UI Designer working on a cross-functional squad, collaborating with developers, product owners, and product managers to deliver optimal solutions for both business needs and user experience across multiple product teams. Pioneered AI-powered design workflows and conducted extensive user research to validate product decisions.

Why This Project Mattered

This wasn't just about redesigning features, it was about proving a new way of working. Could we use AI tools to move faster without sacrificing quality? Could we validate product decisions with research at scale? Could we build features that users actually wanted, not just what stakeholders requested?

The answer: yes. But it required challenging assumptions, testing rigorously, and sometimes saying "the data says otherwise" to senior stakeholders.

The Discovery Journey:

Initially explored Figma Make for creating interactive prototypes. It was helpful for basic prototyping, but limited. Then I discovered Claude, and everything changed. Claude isn't just better than Figma Make, it's fundamentally more powerful and complex.

From Figma Make to Claude: Where Figma Make could create simple prototypes, Claude revolutionized the entire process by enabling:

  • Generating complete, production ready React components with TypeScript and full type safety
  • Building entire responsive websites with working functionality and interactive features
  • Creating accessible UI elements that meet WCAG 2.2 AA standards automatically
  • Integrating seamlessly with design systems through MCP (Model Context Protocol)
  • Iterating and refining based on natural language feedback, no design handoff needed
  • Handling complex business logic, state management, and data flows
  • Producing Storybook documentation with interaction states and accessibility checks

MCP Integration, The Game Changer: Model Context Protocol connected Claude with our design system, component libraries, and development tools. This wasn't just about AI generating code, it was about Claude understanding our specific standards, tokens, and patterns. Every component Claude creates is automatically compliant with our design system.

Why Claude Changes Everything: Traditional design tools like Figma are for creating static representations of interfaces. Claude creates the actual interface. You describe what you want, Claude builds it. You refine with conversation, Claude updates it. This isn't just faster, it's a completely different paradigm. Designers become builders, not just visualizers.

Global Search Redesign

Challenge: Users struggled to discover and effectively use search filters, impacting their ability to find people, documents, and organizations efficiently.

Research conducted: Two usability studies with 15 participants each, testing Spotlight Search and filter usage patterns using UserZoom (unmoderated) and Marvin for synthesis.

Research Findings
90%
Typed directly without using filters
53%
Preferred post-search filtering
33%
Didn't notice filters at all
73%
Expected filter flexibility
Core Insight: When research revealed that 90% of users typed directly into search without using filters, I didn't just redesign the filters... I asked why users avoided them in the first place. That curiosity led to redesigning the entire information hierarchy to better match mental models. Spotlight Search is effective as a fallback, but not yet a trusted first choice. Typing first dominates, and filters are most valuable as refinement tools.

Design solution: Pioneered a new AI-powered workflow by building components directly in Claude with Storybook, rather than traditional Figma to specs approach. Established proper information hierarchy, improved filter visibility, and designed for post-search refinement as the primary flow while allowing pre-search options for power users.

Impact: Reduced user hesitation by making search more discoverable and filters more intuitive. Developers worked directly from functioning code and specifications, accelerating implementation.

What I Learned from Global Search
Research at scale changed how I design

The shift: Testing with 30 users taught me that assumptions are dangerous. What stakeholders want does not equal what users need.

Example: Stakeholders wanted prominent filters upfront. Research showed 90% of users typed directly without using filters. We prioritized post-search refinement instead.

Lesson: Data gives you confidence to push back. "I think" loses to "the research shows." This project taught me to advocate with evidence, not opinions.

Micro Front-End (MFE) Research & Testing

Challenge: Explore whether micro front-ends surfaced within the Feed via collapsible side panels would support managers in making decisions quickly and confidently.

Research conducted: 30 participants across 2 test rounds using Figma Make prototypes and UserZoom (unmoderated testing), synthesized in Marvin.

Top Insights
Right Panel Structure Works Well

Users liked being able to preview or interact with content without losing context. Validated that collapsible MFE structures reduce context switching.

"I really like that you don't lose your place"
Copilot Needs Stronger Predictability

~50% of users unclear on what would happen when interacting with Copilot suggestions.

"Not sure if this will take me somewhere or suggest something"
Clear Visual Hierarchy Reduced Cognitive Load

Most users described interface as "tidy," "clean," and "easy to scan."

"Nice typography. I know what to read first"

Design solution: Created Figma Make prototypes and performed usability testing to validate the approach. Iterated on unclear language in Test 2, refined Copilot interaction patterns, and validated that collapsible MFE structures reduce context switching.

Impact: Successfully validated that panel-based structure aligns with success criteria. No major navigation errors or usability drop-offs. MFE right panel behavior confirmed as effective for quick decision tasks.

What I Learned from MFE Research
When users validate your approach

The win: 30 participants, zero major usability issues. The collapsible side panel structure worked beautifully.

What I learned: When users describe your interface as "tidy," "clean," and "easy to scan," you've achieved visual hierarchy. But...

The caveat: Copilot AI interactions were confusing to 50% of users. Predictability matters more than cleverness. We had to add clearer affordances about what would happen when users clicked AI suggestions.

Product thinking: Success isn't "no errors," it's "users feel confident." Metrics don't tell the full story.

Feed Archive & Restore

Challenge: Determine how users discover and interact with Archive and Restore functions in Feed, and evaluate clarity of interface elements.

Research conducted: 17 unmoderated participants with mixed familiarity levels, analyzing behavioral patterns through UserZoom.

Key Metrics
70%
Used dedicated back button correctly
100%
Successfully completed restore flow
14%
Clicked unrelated elements (minimal confusion)
Key Insight: "Restore to feed" label is universally understood and executed confidently, but "Feed" label creates false affordance as users expect it to be navigable.

Design solution: Improved archive action visibility, refined label clarity, and added hover tooltips for better icon recognition.

Impact: Streamlined archive/restore workflow with clear user expectations and minimal confusion points.

What I Learned from Feed Archive
The false affordance trap

The problem: "Feed" label created false affordance, users expected it to be clickable/navigable when it was just a label.

Insight: Labels that look like buttons cause confusion. Visual hierarchy isn't just about size, it's about affordance signals.

Fix: Changed "Feed" from looking like a nav element to clearly being a descriptor. Small change, big impact on clarity.

Takeaway: Users bring expectations from other interfaces. If something looks interactive, it better be interactive. Otherwise, change how it looks.
