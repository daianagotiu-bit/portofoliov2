TicketPal

Loyalty rewards mobile app for UK railway passengers

iOS & Android Transportation / Loyalty 4 months

Product Vision & Business Context

The Business Challenge: UK railway companies faced declining customer loyalty and needed a way to reward frequent travelers while collecting valuable journey data. The loyalty program existed, but lacked a digital touchpoint that made rewards tangible and engaging.

Product Strategy: Transform an abstract points system into a motivating, visual experience that encourages habit formation. The app needed to make every journey feel rewarding while building a data foundation for personalized offers.

Target Users: Frequent commuters (3+ journeys/week), business travelers, and occasional passengers who value rewards. Ages 25 to 55, tech comfortable, motivated by progress and exclusivity.

Design Challenges & Solutions
Making rewards tangible and motivating

Challenge: Create a points system that feels rewarding and encourages repeat travel

Solution: Clear visual progress indicators, tiered rewards system, and instant point notifications after journeys.

Simplifying point redemption

Challenge: Make it easy for passengers to understand and use their points

Solution: Streamlined redemption flow with clear point values, visual catalog of rewards, and one tap redemption.

Building trust and transparency

Challenge: Users need to trust the points tracking and redemption process

Solution: Complete journey history, real-time point updates, and clear terms for earning and spending.

Key Features Designed

Points Dashboard

Clear overview of earned points, tier status, and available rewards

Journey Tracking

Automatic journey detection with instant point crediting after travel

Rewards Catalog

Browse and redeem points for tickets, upgrades, and partner offers

Tier Progression

Visual tier system showing benefits and progress towards next level

Technology Stack

Technology: React Native

Responsibilities

Concept & Visual Design

Created comprehensive visual direction for loyalty rewards experience

UX Flow Design

Designed user journeys for earning, tracking, and redeeming points

UI Screens

Developed detailed screens for dashboard, rewards, and journey history

Interactive Prototype

Built prototype to validate loyalty program flows and interactions

Tools

Figma, Adobe Illustrator, Miro

Outcome & Impact

Successfully delivered a loyalty rewards app that increased customer engagement with the railway service. The clear points visualization and streamlined redemption flow made the loyalty program feel accessible and rewarding. Passengers reported improved satisfaction with the transparent points tracking and visual tier progression system.
What I Learned & Challenges
Gamification requires careful balance

Learning: Too much visual reward celebration felt overwhelming in early prototypes. Users wanted to feel rewarded but not distracted. I learned that subtle, meaningful feedback works better than flashy animations.

What I changed: Simplified point celebration animations and moved detailed rewards info to a dedicated section rather than interrupting the main flow.

Point expiration caused confusion

Challenge: Initial designs didn't clearly communicate when points would expire, leading to user frustration in testing.

Solution: Added prominent expiration warnings 30 days before points expired, with clear calls to action to redeem. This transparency built trust.

Product thinking shift

Insight: I learned to think beyond "make it pretty" to "does this drive the business goal?" Every design decision needed to either increase journey frequency, boost engagement, or encourage point redemption. This project taught me to advocate for metrics driven design decisions with stakeholders.

TripWizz

Transportation application with AI travel assistant

iOS & Android Business Transportation 3 months

Product Vision & Business Context

The Business Problem: Business travelers faced significant stress during journey disruptions... delays, cancellations, platform changes, with no immediate support or alternative suggestions. This led to missed meetings and frustrated customers.

Product Opportunity: Create a proactive travel companion that doesn't just inform users of problems, but helps them solve them in real-time. The AI assistant needed to feel helpful and intelligent, not robotic.

Success Metrics: Reduce customer support calls during disruptions by 40%, increase user confidence ratings by 30%, achieve 4+ star app store rating focusing on "helpfulness during problems."

Key Features Designed

Trip Planning

Create and manage travel itineraries with ease

Real-Time Updates

Notify users about delays, cancellations, and trip related issues

Disruption Reporting

Users can detect and report 7 types of trip disruptions in real-time

AI Assistant

Chatbot providing guidance and support during disruptions

Visual Appeal

Engaging, modern design combining illustrations for friendly feeling

Technology & Tools

Technology stack: Flutter

Tools: Figma, Adobe Illustrator, Zeplin, Axure, Miro

Responsibilities

  • Concept and visual design
  • High level navigation concept
  • UI screens
  • UI screens specifications
  • Interactive prototypes

Outcome

Final design received positive feedback for practicality and ease of use. Users appreciated clear navigation and visual appeal. Despite tight deadline, delivered quality UI design meeting client expectations with engaging illustrations and modern design elements.
What I Learned & What Didn't Work
AI assistant expectations vs reality

Challenge: Users expected the AI assistant to solve problems automatically ("just book me on the next train"), but technical constraints required more user input than ideal.

What I learned: Set clear expectations early. I added onboarding screens explaining what the assistant could and couldn't do, reducing frustration. This taught me that product education is part of good UX.

Tight timeline forced smart compromises

Reality check: 3 month timeline meant I couldn't do extensive user research. I had to rely on competitive analysis and stakeholder interviews.

Lesson: Learned to validate design decisions with quick prototype tests rather than full research studies. Sometimes "good enough, shipped on time" beats "perfect but late."

Disruption reporting, powerful but underused

The problem: The 7 disruption types feature was complex to design but early feedback showed users rarely reported issues themselves, they just wanted to see what others reported.

Product pivot: Shifted focus from "user reports disruptions" to "user sees crowd-sourced disruption data." This was a key moment in understanding the difference between what stakeholders request vs what users actually need.

CityGo

Urban mobility mobile application

iOS & Android Transportation 3 months

Product Vision & Business Context

The Market Opportunity: Urban commuters juggled multiple apps for bus, metro, and bike sharing. Fragmented experiences led to confusion, missed connections, and preference for private cars over public transport.

Product Vision: Create the "super app" for urban mobility, one interface for all transport modes, with smart routing that considers real-time conditions and personal preferences. Make public transport feel as convenient as ordering a ride share.

Business Goals: Increase public transport usage by 25%, reduce car trips in city center by 15%, establish platform as the default transport app (80% weekly active users among target demographic).

Key Innovation: Eco-statistics gamification to make sustainable choices feel rewarding, not preachy. Turn environmental impact into a personal achievement system.

Design Challenges & Solutions
Complex data integration

Challenge: Combining multiple data sources (bus, metro, bike sharing) into one intuitive interface

Solution: Developed modular UI system to handle dynamic content and real-time updates efficiently.

User trust and reliability

Challenge: Ensuring users trust displayed information (arrival times, delays)

Solution: Implemented clear information hierarchy with familiar color cues (green for on-time, red for delays).

Accessibility and usability

Challenge: Making app inclusive for users with different abilities

Solution: Followed WCAG guidelines, ensured contrast ratios, large tap targets, and voice over compatibility.

Key Features Designed

Live Route Tracking

Users see vehicle locations and estimated arrival times in real time

Smart Recommendations

App suggests optimal routes based on current traffic and user preferences

Integrated Payments

Unified system for buying tickets across multiple transport modes

Eco Statistics

Gamified insights into CO₂ savings, encouraging sustainable commuting

Technology Stack

Technology: React Native

Responsibilities

  • Concept and visual design
  • High level navigation concept
  • UI screens
  • UI specifications
  • Interactive prototypes

Tools

Figma, Adobe Illustrator, Zeplin, Miro

Outcome

The app significantly improved the commuting experience by centralizing travel data and offering real-time accuracy. Users reported increased satisfaction due to clear navigation, reliable information, and modern look and feel. The gamified eco-statistics added engaging touch, motivating users to choose greener transport options.
What I Learned & Honest Reflections
Multi-modal routing is harder than it looks

The complexity: Combining bus, metro, and bike data meant designing for edge cases... what if the bike station is full? What if metro is down?

What worked: Always showing 2 to 3 alternative routes gave users confidence. If one mode failed, they had options.

What I'd improve: The initial version didn't handle "walking distance" preferences well. Some users got recommendations requiring 15 minute walks, which killed adoption. Learning: validate assumptions about what users consider "acceptable" effort.

Eco-stats, the surprise winner

Unexpected insight: I initially thought eco-statistics would be a "nice to have" feature. But user testing revealed it was the most emotionally engaging part of the app.

Why it worked: People want to feel like they're making a difference. Seeing "You've saved 12kg CO₂ this month" made public transport feel like a personal achievement, not a compromise.

Product lesson: Sometimes the "delight" feature becomes the core value prop. Stay open to what users actually respond to, not just what you planned.

Real-time data reliability trust issues

The hard truth: When arrival times were wrong (which happened due to API issues), users lost trust fast. One bad experience could kill app usage.

Solution: Added confidence indicators ("High confidence" vs "Estimated") so users knew when to trust the data. Also showed "Last updated 2min ago" timestamps.

Biggest learning: In product design, managing expectations is as important as delivering features. Transparency builds trust even when things aren't perfect.
